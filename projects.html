Automated Pulse Tool Tester at Atlas Copco

Sep. 2023 - May 2025

University of Colorado Denver

Hardware Loading and Disposal System

Designed and built an automated hardware loading system for the testing and validation of an Atlas Copco pulse tool. A linear rail moves a load cell test rig between stations that sequentially load a bolt, nut, and washer, apply torque from the pulse tool, and read clamping force. The system enables direct correlation between torque (from a torque spindle) and resulting clamping force across varying hardware types, sizes, and lubrication conditions. An automated disposal mechanism removes the used hardware after each test, allowing for continuous testing with a fresh set of hardware.

Fluids Research

Sep. 2023 - May 2025

University of Colorado Denver

Testing Setup

As pictured, three vibrating cylinders were mounted within a rail system located inside the wind tunnel. The oscillations were measured with a laser displacement sensor. The air speed within the vortex-shedding region was measured with a hotwire anemometer. Using FFT algorithms, the natural frequency of oscillations and vortex shedding were computed, allowing for comparisons to see if the cylinders were facing vortex-induced vibrations (VIV).

CFD and Wind Tunnel Airfoil Comparison

Aug. 2023 - Dec. 2023

NACA 4412 Airfoil

Airfoil Pressure Taps

A NACA 4412 airfoil with 18 pressure taps was tested at a Reynolds number of 2.5 × 105 at various angles-of-attack. The pressure taps are distributed evenly, with 9 along the upper surface and 9 along the lower surface. Pressure coefficients were generated along the upper and lower surfaces, and numerical integration was used to generate the lift coefficients.

2D Test Domain

A 2D C-type computational domain was created in ANSYS around the NACA 4412 airfoil. A structured mesh was created with edge sizing refinements utilized near the airfoil surface. The sizing refinements increase the accuracy of boundary layer behavior on the airfoil’s surface. The velocity inlet condition was applied to the top, bottom, and left surfaces of the domain. A pressure outlet condition was used on the right wall. The properties of air were modified to match the experimental values. To vary the angles of attack, the incoming flow direction was modified according to the angle, and the components of lift and drag force were decomposed using sine and cosine functions. This ensured the definitions of lift and drag stayed aligned with the direction of the freestream air.

Experimental and CFD Comparison

The comparison plot was created by graphing the experimental and CFD data on Excel. As seen, the CFD and experimental results are similar, but do not match up exactly. At higher angles of attack, the CFD simulation underpredicts the lift coefficient when compared to the experimental data. This is likely due to limitations within the turbulence model used in ANSYS Fluent, which may not accurately capture the flow separation and stall onset. Additionally, the mesh resolution could play a factor in the differences seen.

Rocket Lynx

Jan. 2023 - May 2023

University of Colorado Denver

Rocket Ignition Device

A rocket ignition device was designed, manufactured, and soldered. The device features a key switch and a red button. Following the insertion of the key, the device could be turned on and set to an armed position. From here, the pressing of the button would connect the circuit, allowing for a 9V battery to deliver current to our initiators, resulting in a small explosion that would light the rocket engine.

Test Day

The hybrid rocket propulsion engine was tested. Using a load cell and a DAQ system, a peak thrust of 73 pound-force (lbf) was measured.
